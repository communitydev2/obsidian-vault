### Making Video Games in 2025 (without an engine) – Summary - https://www.noelberry.ca/posts/making_games_in_2025/

**Core Thesis**

Noel Berry argues that in 2025, building games without a large commercial engine (such as Unity or Unreal) is not only viable, but often preferable for indie developers. For many 2D or stylised projects, a lightweight custom stack built from open-source tools can offer more control, stability, and enjoyment than relying on a monolithic engine.

**Philosophy and Motivation**

Large engines introduce unnecessary complexity, features developers may not need, and risks tied to corporate decisions or breaking updates. For solo developers or small teams, the effort required to deeply learn a major engine can rival the effort needed to build a focused, custom foundation.

Berry values ownership, simplicity, and creative freedom. Building tools can be part of the fun rather than a burden, especially when tailored precisely to a project’s needs.

**Language Choice**

C# is his preferred language in 2025. It strikes a balance between performance and accessibility, offers modern language features, and benefits from improvements like hot reload and ahead-of-time compilation.

**Core Technology Stack**

Instead of a full engine, Berry builds on lightweight libraries:

- SDL3 for windowing, input, and rendering, with strong cross-platform support.
- A small custom C# abstraction layer built on top of SDL.
- FMOD for advanced audio (the only proprietary component in his setup).

This modular approach replaces a single monolithic engine with carefully chosen building blocks.

**Tools and Workflow**

For tools and editors, he uses:

- Dear ImGui for fast, in-game editor interfaces without heavyweight UI frameworks.
- External level editors such as LDtk or Tiled where appropriate.

He prefers simple, practical workflows over complex asset pipelines.

**Asset and Data Strategy**

Assets are loaded simply, often upfront or on demand. Instead of elaborate engine pipelines, he writes custom scripts to preprocess assets during compilation when necessary. The focus is clarity and control rather than automation for its own sake.

**Platform Support**

Concerns about C# and console deployment are addressed through ahead-of-time compilation, allowing native builds without a traditional engine. SDL3’s broad platform support further strengthens portability across desktop and console environments.

**When This Approach Makes Sense**

This workflow is especially well-suited to:

- 2D games  
- Stylised or mechanically focused projects  
- Small teams or solo developers  
- Developers who value control and minimalism  

For larger 3D projects or bigger teams, full-featured engines may still make sense. If using a full engine, Berry recommends open-source options like Godot for those wanting to avoid proprietary lock-in.

**Bottom Line**

In 2025, making games without a traditional engine is entirely feasible. With modern languages, open-source libraries, and better tooling, indie developers can build streamlined, maintainable, and highly customised workflows — often with less friction than large commercial engines.

The key trade-off is responsibility: you gain control and flexibility, but you also own the tooling decisions. For the right developer and the right project, that trade is worth it.
